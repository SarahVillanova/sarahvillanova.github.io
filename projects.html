My Research

My research focuses on the astrophysical processes that shape the lives and deaths of stars, especially through complex binary interactions and exotic compact objects. I use numerical simulations and theoretical modeling to study how these systems evolve, radiate, and ultimately influence the universe around them.

Common Envelope Simulations

The common envelope phase is one of the least understood but most crucial stages in binary star evolution. During this phase, one star expands and engulfs its companion, creating a shared gaseous envelope. This process can lead to the formation of close compact binaries, such as white dwarf–neutron star or black hole–neutron star systems, which are key sources of gravitational waves.

In my Ph.D. work, I perform 3D hydrodynamic simulations of common envelope evolution to understand how energy and angular momentum are transferred within the system. These simulations help constrain the physical conditions that determine whether the envelope is successfully ejected or whether the stars merge. My goal is to connect simulation outcomes with observations of post-common-envelope binaries and gravitational wave progenitors.

Emission Models for SGRs/AXPs

In addition to binary interactions, I’m interested in highly magnetized neutron stars known as Soft Gamma Repeaters (SGRs) and Anomalous X-ray Pulsars (AXPs). These objects are thought to be magnetars — neutron stars powered by their extreme magnetic fields rather than by accretion or rotation.

My work on emission models explores how the magnetic field geometry and surface processes of magnetars produce their characteristic X-ray and gamma-ray emission. By comparing theoretical predictions with observational data, we can gain insight into their magnetic evolution, crustal dynamics, and the mechanisms behind their bursts and flares.
